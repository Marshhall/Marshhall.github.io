What is this?

"Assume a spherical cow" is one of the classic jokes in physics. The story goes that a farmer asks a physicist to help improve milk production. The physicist disappears for weeks and finally returns with a solution: "Consider a spherical cow of uniform density, in a vacuum..."

The joke gently mocks the way theoretical physics often replaces messy reality with simple, elegant models. In truth, these approximations are incredibly powerful: much of modern physics has been built on them.

A mathematician might see the cow differently. To them, a cow has a mouth, two nostrils, and, well… one more opening at the other end. In topology, that makes it equivalent to a sphere with three handles (or, if you prefer, three conjoined doughnuts).

Both the physicist and the mathematician are right in their own way. The physicist simplified the cow, while the mathematician counted every hole. Even that one. They simply disagree on which parts of the cow are worth keeping.
