Transcendental Dynamics: The Complex Exponential Family

During this summer I've had the chance to participate in the Barcelona Introduction to Mathematical Research programme. Here, I have been able to carry out a research project on Transcendental Dynamics involving the complex exponential family. In this post, I will explain in detail what the project is all about.

Some Preliminaries

We know that for a function $f: \mathbb{C} \to \mathbb{C}$, the orbit of its critical points and its assymptotic values determine (qualitatively) the dynamics of this function. For instance, $f(z) = e^z$ has no critical points and one assymptotic value, namely $z = 0$, since taking any curve $\gamma: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ that tends to $\infty$ such that $\text{Re}(\gamma(t))$ tends to $-\infty$ as $t \to \infty$ will satisfy that $\lim_{t \to \infty} e^{\gamma(t)} = 0$.

We will study the complex exponential family, namely $E_{\lambda} (z) := \lambda e^z$, where $\lambda, z \in \mathbb{C}$, for different values of the parameter $\lambda$. We have already seen that this function only has one assymptotic value: $z = 0$. If we fix $\lambda = 1$, the orbit of the point $z = 0$ is the following: $$ 0 \to 1 \to e \to e^e \to e^{e^e} \to \dots $$ That is, $E_1^n(0)$ goes to $\infty$ as $n$ gets larger and larger. From this we can already conclude that the Julia set of $E_1$ is the entire complex plane: $J(E_1) = \mathbb{C}$.

The Problem

We have seen that the dynamics for $\lambda = 1$ are quite dramatic, since we cannot have, for instance, a periodic orbit. What happens now if we change, just a little bit, the value of the parameter $\lambda$? Will the dynamics be qualitatively the same, i.e. conjugate? Or will we have a completely different behavior?
