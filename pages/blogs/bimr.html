Transcendental Dynamics: The Complex Exponential Family

During this summer I had the chance to participate in the Barcelona Introduction to Mathematical Research programme. As part of the programme I carried out a small research project on Transcendental Dynamics involving the complex exponential family. In this post I explain the main ideas behind the project.

Some Preliminaries

We know that for a function $f: \mathbb{C} \to \mathbb{C}$, the orbit of its critical points and asymptotic values largely determine the qualitative dynamics of the function.

For instance, $f(z) = e^z$ has no critical points and one asymptotic value, namely $z = 0$. Taking any curve $\gamma : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{C}$ that tends to $\infty$ such that $\text{Re}(\gamma(t)) \to -\infty$ as $t \to \infty$, we obtain

$$\lim_{t\to\infty} e^{\gamma(t)} = 0.$$

We study the complex exponential family

$$E_\lambda(z) := \lambda e^z,$$

where $\lambda, z \in \mathbb{C}$, for different values of the parameter $\lambda$.

As we have seen, this function has a single asymptotic value: $z=0$. If we fix $\lambda = 1$, the orbit of $0$ is

$$0 \to 1 \to e \to e^e \to e^{e^e} \to \dots$$

In other words, $E_1^n(0)$ diverges to $\infty$ as $n$ grows. From this we can already conclude that the Julia set of $E_1$ is the entire complex plane:

$$J(E_1) = \mathbb{C}.$$

The Problem

We have seen that the dynamics for $\lambda = 1$ are quite dramatic: there cannot be, for instance, a periodic orbit.

What happens if we change the parameter $\lambda$ slightly? Do the dynamics remain qualitatively the same — that is, conjugate to the previous case — or does the behaviour change completely?
