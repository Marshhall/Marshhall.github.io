Book Recommendations

This is a list of resources I have found very useful during my time at university. They are mostly books I have enjoyed or amazing websites to learn new things.

Mathematics

Vector Calculus, Linear Algebra and Differential Forms
by John Hubbard and Barbara Burke Hubbard

I was lucky to find this book through the awesome YouTube channel 3Blue1Brown. I would recommend it to anyone who wants to feel what it feels like to do math. It has a unique and wonderfully deep and intuitive way to convey difficult concepts.

Visual Complex Analysis
by Tristan Needham

Just amazing. I have always been fond of explaining something visually if possible, it just sticks for much longer. I would supplement this book with others, since it is also important to learn the formal details of this discipline. Ahlfors or Conway are among the best options.

Visual Differential Geometry and Forms
by Tristan Needham

Written by the same author as Visual Complex analysis. Just amazing. Again, I would not use this book as the only reference to learn differential geometry, but it provides useful insights and intuition on the subject. To get more into the formal weeds, take a look at the next book.

Introduction to Smooth Manifolds
by John M. Lee

One of the clearest routes into differential geometry: clean definitions, strong structure, and lots of guidance. Great as a serious "learn it properly" reference that still reads well.

Mathematical Methods of Classical Mechanics
by V. I. Arnold

A beautiful view of classical mechanics through geometry and with the guidance of one of my favorite authors. Symplectic ideas, Hamiltonian structure, and the "conceptual backbone" that shows up everywhere in modern math/physics.

Ordinary Differential Equations
by V. I. Arnold

Another one from the genius of Arnold. Short, sharp, and concept-driven. More like "how to think about ODEs" than a catalog of techniques. Great for intuition and qualitative understanding.

The Princeton Companion to Mathematics
edited by Timothy Gowers

A huge, high-quality map of modern mathematics: short articles, big-picture context, and tons of doors to open. Great for browsing and for finding "what should I learn next?"

Physics

The Feynman Lectures on Physics
by Richard P. Feynman (and others)

I do not even know where to start. Genius! These lectures are full of insights, and will help you understand concepts from the ground up. Feyman makes difficult concepts feel almost obvious sometimes, and just for this reason, it's worth it. However, he also knows how to get deep into the mathematics of it when needed.

Spacetime Physics: Introduction to Special Relativity
by Edwin F. Taylor, John Archibald Wheeler

This is the best introduction to special relativity I have ever seen, and with great problems too! The human-crow interaction will forever be one of my favorites methods of teaching. One of the best "learn by thinking" books in the subject.

Gravitation
by Charles W. Misner, Kip S. Thorne, John Archibald Wheeler

The classic big-reference for general relativity: encyclopedic, deep, and packed with insight. Written by geniuses. Not a "quick read," but an amazing place to grow into when you want the full story.

Principles of Quantum Mechanics
by R. Shankar

A classic for learning quantum mechanics with real mathematical backbone. Strong explanations, lots of worked reasoning, and a very "complete course" feel. I also enjoyed the discussion on the postulates of QM. Here's Shankar himself introducing the subject!

Quantum Physics of Atoms, Molecules, Solids, Nuclei, and Particles
by Robert Eisberg, Robert Resnick

IMO, this book does a wonderful job providing physical intuition for phenomena in QM. For instance, the addition of angular momenta or the effects of the symmetries of the wave function in terms of how close (in space) can two particles be. Really nice when you want the "physics landscape" and lots of concrete examples.

Statistical Mechanics
by R. K. Pathria, Paul D. Beale

Clear intuitive and formal development of ensembles, quantum statistics, and many core results that show up everywhere in physics. Great when you want both derivations and a reliable reference.

Lectures by Walter Lewin

Just amazing. He has three series, mainly on Classical Mechanics, Electromagnetism, and Waves. If you are struggling with some elementary (as a synonym for fundamental, not easy), or just for fun, then you should definitely check them out.

Introduction to Electrodynamics
by David J. Griffiths

In this book, Griffiths brilliantly conveys both the intuitive and formal aspects of electromagnetism. If you are looking for a gentle, useful, and robust introduction to this topic, then this is your book.
